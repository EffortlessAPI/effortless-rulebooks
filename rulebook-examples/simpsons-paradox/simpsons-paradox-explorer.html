What is Simpson's Paradox?

A statistical phenomenon where a trend that appears in several groups of data disappears — or reverses — when those groups are combined. It occurs when a confounding variable (one that affects both who receives each treatment and how well they do) is unevenly distributed across the groups being compared.

The classic example: in the 1986 kidney stone study, Treatment A cures a higher fraction of patients in every size category — but Treatment B looks better in the pooled numbers. Why? Because A was preferentially given to the harder cases. The pooled rate conflates treatment effectiveness with case difficulty.

What this tool shows

Five studies — two real, two synthetic controls, one counterfactual — are run through a DAG of derived formulas. Every number you see is computed live from the raw (successes, cases) leaf data embedded in this file:

  • Study Overview — scatter plot of all studies on the distortion plane. Cards show per-study metrics including the new continuous measures.
  • Stratum Breakdown — per-stratum bars vs pooled dashed lines. When bars go one way but the dashed line goes the other, you're looking at the paradox.
  • Allocation Weights — how each treatment's cases are spread across strata. The allocation bias is the confounder's fingerprint as a signed number.
  • Interactive Sandbox — sliders for raw counts. Watch the reversal verdict flip in real time as you shift allocation.

The four distortion types

Type A — Full reversal Type B — Sign flip (partial) Type C — Compressed, no flip Type D — Neutral

Beyond the classic binary IsReversal, this model derives AllocationDistortion — the magnitude of how far the allocation has bent the pooled signal — and IsSignFlip, which correctly classifies Berkeley 1973 as paradoxical even though its per-department winner isn't unanimous.
