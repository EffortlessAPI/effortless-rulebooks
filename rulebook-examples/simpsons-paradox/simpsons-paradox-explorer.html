What is Simpson's Paradox?

A statistical phenomenon where a trend that appears in every subgroup disappears — or reverses — when the subgroups are combined. It occurs when a confounding variable influences both who receives each treatment and how well they do, creating an allocation imbalance that bends the pooled conclusion.

The classic example: in the 1986 kidney stone study, Treatment A cures a higher fraction in every size category — yet Treatment B looks better in the pooled numbers. Why? Because the harder cases were preferentially routed to Treatment A, dragging its pooled rate down.

The four distortion types (loops 19–21)

Type A — Full reversal Type B — Sign flip (partial) Type C — Compressed, no flip Type D — Neutral

Beyond the classic binary IsReversal (unanimity criterion), this model derives AllocationDistortion — the scalar magnitude of how far the pooled signal has been bent — and IsSignFlip, which correctly classifies Berkeley 1973 as a Simpson-type paradox even though its per-department winner is not unanimous. The type taxonomy is geometry-first: no causal story required to classify.
